# Literature Review Devlog

## 1. Togelius et al. Search-based Procedural Content Generation: A Taxomy and Survey
**Date:** 6.15.26  
**Synthesis:** Togelius et al. establishes a comphrehensive taxomy for Search-based Procedural Content Generation (SBPCG) specifically focusing on games wherein it discusses why procedural content generation is devised (primarily to save memory ("unexpanded" content), development resources especially in the context of hardware improvements and player level of expected detail, the expanding of game mechanics and the augumenting of human imagination). It further goes on to categorize how the content is generated comparing the pros and cons of different generation concepts as well as how it is then represented within games(through weapons, levels, puzzles etc.). The quality/fitness of the content is then evaluated in a broader context as well as the broader impacts/concerns. Search-based content generation itself is game design approach wherein an algorithm attempts to continuously optimize content creation based on said fitness functions.

## 2. Yannakakis and Togelius Experience-Driven Procedural Content Generation (Extended Abstract)
**Date:** 6.16.26

**Synthesis:** Yannakakis and Togelius discuss their Experience-Driven Procedural Content Generation Framework, driven by four key components, namely Player Experience Modeling (either Subjectively, considering only the player's view of their experience, Objectively, through unconcious physical responses and the like albeit very intrusive and low practicality, or through Gameplay-based Player Experience Modeling inspired  by theoretical principles or existing player metrics), Content Quality (judged by level of playability, assignment of "content values", or player interactions with content), Content Representation (through direct encoding and indirect encoding), and the Content Generator. They, then go on to discuss this framework in action through their Super Mario Personalized Level Generator. 

## 3. Yannakakis Affective Game Computing: A Survey 
**Date:** 6.21.26

**Synthesis:** Yannakakis maps the intersection of Affective Computing (AC) and Game Research through the lens of "Affective Computing", essentially exploring how games can elicit, sense, detect and adapt to human emotion. The Affective loop described has four key ideas, Game Affect elicitation (how games are designed to elicit specific emotional states), Game Affect Sensing (focusing on hardware and physiological signals), Game Affect Detection and Modeling (focusing on computational methods like machine learning), Game Affect Adaptation (focusing on procedural content generation and the like, how a game can adapt to sustain engagement). The survey further reviews available annotation tools, affective game datasets, and sensing technologies while identifying several research gaps, including the reliance on intrusive biosensors, the lack of standardized datasets and annotation protocols, limited deployment in commercial games, and ethical and privacy challenges. The authors argue that future progress may be reliant on more scalable adaptation capable of real-world use, more practical/less intrusive sensing technology, and richer multimodal data. 

## 4. Summerville et al. Gemini: Bidirectional Generation & Analysis via ASP  
**Date:** 6.23.26

**Synthesis:** This paper introduces a system called Gemini designed to bridge the gap between game mechanics and the game's meaning by using Answer Set Programming (ASP), giving it the unique ability to understand the games it generated (compared to older models like Game-o-matic) through bidirectional generation. What it does is take an existing system's rules as input and performs a "proceduralist reading" essentially deducing higher level thematic meanings (game --> interpretation), which it then combines with a user defined target/message to generate a full set of playable rules to guarantee adherence to said target reading. Gemini uses a DSL (domain-specific language) called Cygnus, in comparison to the usual VGDL, to encompass a broader domain of constructs. Further, at its time it was the first game generation system able to analyze its own rules (bidirectionality) to ensure no clash between mechanics and thematic messages. Gemini is a solver-based pcg game generator. 

## 5. Summerville et al. Procedural Content Generation via Machine Learning
**Date:** 6.24.26

**Synthesis:** Summerville et al. discusses procedural content generation with machine learning or PCGML (machine learning models trained on game content specifically) in the context of functional game content. The authors categorize the application of PCGML under a couple distinct roles including saving codification of design knowledge and intents in autonomous, lowering barrier to entry or autocompletion in human-machine design, repair, critique and evaluations. The survey then proceeds to discuss PCGML methodologies covering model types (Markov Chains and n-grams for sequential data, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs/LSTMs) for learning structural patterns over time, Autoencoders (VAEs) for latent space exploration, and Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) for generating complex game content) as well as the problem of data aquisition (given that video game datasets are scarce and small with no standardized evaluation benchmarks, forcing few-shot learning or data augumentation techniques). It ends with important open problems in the research and application of PCGML including steering to specific design constraints, representation of data, learning on different abstractions of content, evaluation of content, designer input, use as a mechanic and better learning from small datasets. The authors argue that PCGML (because of the nature of games as interactive dynamic systems) may need to learn not only content, but mechanics, dynamics and aesthetics to create a system versus isolated assets. 

## 6. Wang and Kreminski Guiding and Diversifying LLM-Based Story Generation via Answer Set Programming
**Date:** 6.26.26

**Synthesis:** The authors discuss LLM-based story generation, introducing a methodology in which they generate a large and diverse set of "solutions" encoding multiple viable sequences of narrative goals (using ASP) and then at random picking an outline from said set and generating a complete textual story (by way of LLM conditioning generation). The generation of diverse story outcomes can be described as passing a set of high level narrative functions (timeline, scenes, parameters for diversification) and constraints (as to prevent invalid partial orderings of narrative functions) to an ASP solver (clingo). During the write phase, an outline, and user input (high level premise) is passed with a dictionary correlating narrative functions to human authored directions for the LLM to follow. To evaluate story diversity, homogeneity scores for paragraphs were calculated and compared. Limitations(systematic human evaluation, controllability) and related work (use of ASP to generate plot events, Luminate, improvements to diversity from a fixed LLM) is discussed as well.

## 7. Osborn et al. Is Your Game Generator Working? Evaluating Gemini, an Intentional Generator
**Date:** 6.26.26

**Synthesis:** Osborn et al. examine the Gemini Game Generator to evaluate how reliably humans interpret its mechanics and higher-order proceduralist arguments. Contextualizing Gemini against abstract game evaluators like Ludi, ANGELINA, and Game-o-matic, the authors explore how games express ideas through rule-based systems (procedural rhetoric), arguing that a baseline of cultural framing is required to make a game's systemic arguments comprehensible. To test Gemini's core claim that players will interpret its games consistently with the system's internal logic, the authors had 76 student participants play three generated games each using a custom web interface. Participants were split into two groups to evaluate dimensional variations: Group A played mechanically similar games with differing thematic meanings to see if players could distinguish intent, while Group B played mechanically distinct games sharing the same design intent to see if identical themes could be recognized across varying systems. To prevent priming, players were provided a persistent list of possible actions rather than explicit instructions for the active game. Each game lasted up to five minutes and was followed by a post-survey measuring player alignment across two core criteria: mechanical understanding, which was evaluated via a multiple-choice checklist of active mechanics scored against an automated answer key mapped to each game's source code, and higher-level interpretations, which were assessed via open-ended free-text questions coded by independent raters alongside a final forced-choice question requiring players to match each game to its intended designer-encoded metaphor
