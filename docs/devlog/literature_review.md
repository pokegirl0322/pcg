# Literature Review Devlog

## 1. Togelius et al. Search-based Procedural Content Generation: A Taxomy and Survey
**Date:** 6.15.26  
**Synthesis:** Togelius et al. establishes a comphrehensive taxomy for Search-based Procedural Content Generation (SBPCG) specifically focusing on games wherein it discusses why procedural content generation is devised (primarily to save memory ("unexpanded" content), development resources especially in the context of hardware improvements and player level of expected detail, the expanding of game mechanics and the augumenting of human imagination). It further goes on to categorize how the content is generated comparing the pros and cons of different generation concepts as well as how it is then represented within games(through weapons, levels, puzzles etc.). The quality/fitness of the content is then evaluated in a broader context as well as the broader impacts/concerns. 

## 2. Yannakakis and Togelius Experience-Driven Procedural Content Generation (Extended Abstract)
**Date:** 6.16.26

**Synthesis:** Yannakis and Togelius discuss their Experience-Driven Procedural Content Generation Framework, driven by four key components, namely Player Experience Modeling (either Subjectively, considering only the player's view of their experience, Objectively, through unconcious physical responses and the like albeit very intrusive and low practicality, or through Gameplay-based Player Experience Modeling inspired  by theoretical principles or existing player metrics), Content Quality (judged by level of playability, assignment of "content values", or player interactions with content), Content Representation (through direct encoding and indirect encoding), and the Content Generator. They, then go on to discuss this framework in action through their Super Mario Personalized Level Generator. 

## 3. Yannakakis Affective Game Computing: A Survey 
**Date:** 6.21.26

**Synthesis:** Yannakakis maps the intersection of Affective Computing (AC) and Game Research through the lens of "Affective Computing", essentially exploring how games can elicit, sense, detect and adapt to human emotion. The Affective loop described has four key ideas, Game Affect elicitation (how games are designed to elicit specific emotional states), Game Affect Sensing (focusing on hardware and physiological signals), Game Affect Detection and Modeling (focusing on computational methods like machine learning), Game Affect Adaptation (focusing on procedural content generation and the like, how a game can adapt to sustain engagement). It catalogs the available academic and industrial game-affect datasets, comparing modalities like facial videos, multi-modal physiological telemetry, and player self-reporting tools, as well as discussing industry and academia uses. Finally, it analyzes the primary hurdles holding back Affective Game Computing from becoming widespread, namely intrustivity, unpredictability, lack of ease of scaling and ethical/privacy concerns. 

## 4. Summerville et al. Gemini: Bidirectional Generation & Analysis via ASP  
**Date:** 6.23.26

**Synthesis:** This paper introduces a system called Gemini designed to bridge the gap between game mechanics and the game's meaning by using Answer Set Programming (ASP), giving it the unique ability to understand the games it generated (compared to older models like Game-o-matic) through bidirectional generation. What it does is take an existing system's rules as input and performs a "proceduralist reading" essentially deducing higher level thematic meanings (game --> interpretation), which it then combines with a user defined target/message to generate a full set of playable rules to guarantee adherence to said target reading. Gemini uses a DSL (domain-specific language) called Cygnus, in comparison to the usual VGDL, to encompass a broader domain of constructs. Further, at its time it was the first game generation system able to analyze its own rules (bidirectionality) to ensure no clash between mechanics and thematic messages.
